I am a Post-Doctoral Fellow at the Immigration Policy Lab in the Department of Humanities, Social and Political Sciences and the Center for Comparative and International Studies at ETH Zurich. Prior to joining the Immigration Policy Lab, I received my PhD in Political Science from Washington University in St. Louis, and was a visiting scholar at the Department of Economics and Business Economics at Aarhus University.

My research focuses primarily on immigrant integration and native attitudes toward immigrants. In particular, my work examines the impact of local contexts -- e.g., the partisanship of municipal office holders, immigrant diversity, and the demographics of immigrants -- on integration and the development of anti-immigrant attitudes. My main project studies the consequences of diversity within immigrant population, showing that when immigrants come from a variety of countries, integration proceeds more smoothly and with less backlash among natives. Another major project studies the role of local politics and the ideology of parties in facilitating integration. I am also generally interested in comparative party politics, causal inference, political economy, political methodology, and policy.

For more information on my work, take a look at my CV, the papers listed below, or my Immigration Policy Lab page.

Peer-Reviewed Publications

  • "How Economic Integration Affects Party Issue Emphases," (with Jeong-Hyun Kim, Matthew Graham and Margit Tavits). 2015. Comparative Political Studies 48(10): 1227-1259.
        Abstract    
    The economic dimension has typically been considered the primary dimension of political party competition. However, parties often rally voters on the basis of non-economic issues. In this article, we argue that integration into global markets and into the European Union (EU) constrains parties' abilities to credibly differentiate themselves on economic issues. Given these constraints, and voters' awareness of them, parties activate other non-economic issues along which to compete. Using data across 49 countries between 1961 and 2010, this study shows that increased economic integration is associated with increased emphasis on non-economic issues during election campaigns. This effect persists over time and remains robust to a number of alternative explanations and modeling strategies. We also explore the proposed causal mechanisms and show that integration is indeed associated with convergence of party positions on economic issues and that all types of parties–not just new and niche parties–respond to integration by politicizing non-economic issues. Finally, we leverage the latest round of EU enlargement to causally identify the effect of integration on party strategies.

Working Papers

  • "Do Natives Oppose Young Men More than Other Immigrants?." Under Review.
        Abstract    
    Many migration events are characterized by a large share of young men among the migrants. How this effects attitudes is unclear: young men have the potential to make substantial economic contributions, meaning attitudes towards them may be more favorable. However, young men may also pose security and cultural threats, exacerbating anti-immigrant attitudes. I conduct a conjoint experiment to a sample of 2,100 Germans, asking them to evaluate groups of immigrants with randomly varying shares of young men. The results show that groups of immigrants with a large share of young male migrants provoke substantial native opposition. Further tests reveal this opposition is primarily due to the security and cultural threats posed by young men; there is no evidence that young men are viewed as having high economic potential.

  • "Dynamic Effects of Electoral Laws." R&R at Journal of Elections, Public Opinion & Parties.
        Abstract    
    A change in electoral laws is expected to substantially alter political outcomes as voters and elites adjust their behavior to new rules. However, testing the causal implications of this theory using electoral reforms has been difficult because election results before a reform are not the appropriate counterfactual for election results after a reform. This article leverages electoral reform in New Zealand and Italy during the 1990's and the synthetic control method to approximate the appropriate counterfactuals: election results in the period after reform, had the reform not occurred. I find that increasing the disproportionality of electoral laws causes party systems to shrink. However, contrary to theoretical expectations, I do not find evidence that the adoption of more proportional electoral laws causes a long term party system expansion.

  • "Judicial Review Timing and Legislative Posturing: Reconsidering the Moral Hazard Problem," (with Matthew Gabel). Under Review.
        Abstract    
    The prospect of judicial review may cause legislators to adjust their behavior to anticipate the court's response. This can serve as a source of moral hazard for legislators. Legislators can pursue irresponsible policies that are potentially electorally valuable and then rely on the court to bail them out by overturning the policy before implementation. We examine this issue by extending a seminal model of judicial review (Fox and Stephenson 2011) to include review both before the implementation of policy - ex ante review - and after the implementation of policy - ex post review. Our analysis shows that the potential deleterious effects of judicial review are reduced when review occurs after policy costs are realized because politicians self-discipline. However, the full welfare implications for voters are complex and each form of review has advantages.

  • "How Partisan Affect Shapes Citizens' Perception of the Political World," (with Margit Tavits). Under Review.
        Abstract    
    We explore how partisan affect shapes citizens' views of party ideology and political competition. We argue that voters' affective ties to parties (both positive and negative) lead them to perceive the ideological positions of those parties as more extreme. Further, when voters are ``affectively polarized," i.e., they strongly like some parties and dislike others, then they are more likely to view politics as high stakes competition, where ideological polarization is rampant, participation is crucial, and electoral outcomes are highly consequential. Using cross-national survey data covering 41 elections in 34 countries, we show that partisan affect indeed impacts perceptions of party ideology and affective polarization alters beliefs about the nature of political competition. Additional tests leverage an exogenous shock to affective polarization resulting from the assassination of a Dutch politician to causally identify the role of partisan affect on voter biases.

  • "Local Political Contexts and Immigrant Integration," (with Anna Piil Damm).
        Abstract    
    The integration of immigrants is one of the most important policy tasks facing politicians today. This is especially true for local politicians, whose decisions can directly impact integration. How do local political contexts impact integration? To credibly identify the effects of municipal party government we leverage micro data from the Danish statistical registers on refugees entering Denmark from 1985-1998 and Denmark???s spatial dispersion policy. Denmark's policy served as a natural experiment, quasi-randomly assigning refugees to municipalities around Denmark and allowing for the identification of the effect of municipality characteristics. We find that assignment to a municipality governed by a left party increases integration, as measured by out-migration from Denmark. Further results show this effect is contingent upon the security of the left's majority in the local council.

  • "Immigrant Diversity, Native Attitudes, and Integration.''
        Abstract    
    Immigrant populations have grown rapidly in both size and diversity in recent decades, making immigrant integration a pressing policy challenge for many developed democracies. Despite the vast research on this topic, we know little about how diversity within immigrant populations impacts integration. I argue that immigrant diversity fosters integration because immigrant diversity reduces anti-immigrant attitudes among native populations. Immigrant diversity reduces anti-immigrant attitudes because it prevents the development and usage of negative stereotypes of immigrants by natives. Using data from over 700 localities in Denmark and Germany covering more than 30 years, I show that integration is more successful when immigrant diversity is higher. Additional analyses show that the positive effect of diversity on integration is stronger with Non-Western immigrants than with Western immigrants and allow me to rule out reverse causality as an explanation of my results. I conduct an original conjoint experiment in Germany to test the main mechanism of my argument, and find that immigrant diversity reduces anti-immigrant attitudes. Overall, my findings show that immigrant diversity has a positive effect on integration and native attitudes, suggesting that policies prioritizing immigrant diversity may have substantial benefits.

  • "The Causal Effect of University Education on Political Attitudes and Behavior,'' (with Dominik Hangartner, Lukas Schmid and Stefan Boes)
        Abstract    
    Does higher education change an individual???s political attitudes and behaviors? Despite myriad studies on this topic, we lack reliable evidence that isolates the causal effect of university education on ideology and participation from bias due to non-random selection into higher education. We identify this effect by leveraging quasi-random variation in university graduation created by entry tests for upper-level secondary schools used in Switzerland. Comparing otherwise similar citizens who, as pupils, narrowly pass or narrowly fail their school entry test, we find that attending and completing a university track moves citizens to the left. Our attitudinal measures encompass both left-right self-positioning and policy preferences, suggesting a comprehensive effect on attitudes. Further analyses show that the ideology effects are larger for more right-wing citizens. We also find that university education increases both formal and informal participation, and that this effect is particularly large among those that already participate at high rates. Overall, our findings confirm the conjecture of previous, correlational studies that university education has a causal effect on political attitudes and behavior.
